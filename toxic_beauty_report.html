Introduction: The Allure and the Alarm

The global cosmetics industry is a multi-billion dollar powerhouse, reflecting a universal desire for self-expression and care. In 2024 alone, it generated an estimated $548 billion in revenue, with Skincare emerging as the largest and fastest-growing category.

However, beneath the glossy packaging and appealing marketing, a critical concern persists: the widespread use of potentially toxic chemicals in products applied daily to our skin, hair, and nails.

This project, "Toxic Beauty," aims to lift the veil on these hidden dangers. By analyzing extensive data, we seek to provide clarity and empower consumers, regulators, and the industry itself to prioritize safety.

The Problem: Beauty's Toxic Secret

The beauty and skincare industry is flourishing, yet this growth often masks a significant issue. Many everyday products – from makeup to lotions to hair dyes – contain chemicals that have been linked through scientific research to adverse health effects. These concerns include:

  • Hormonal Disruption: Interference with the body's endocrine system, potentially impacting development and reproduction.
  • Allergies & Sensitization: Triggering skin reactions, irritation, and long-term sensitivities.
  • Carcinogenicity: Potential links to increased cancer risk based on specific chemical classifications (e.g., by IARC, Prop 65).

While consumer awareness about ingredient safety is rising, navigating the complex world of chemical names and potential risks remains challenging. There's a clear need for accessible, data-driven analysis to help evaluate product safety beyond marketing claims.

This project directly addresses this gap by using real-world data to investigate the prevalence and patterns of hazardous chemicals in cosmetics.

Project Objectives

Our investigation was guided by four key objectives:

1. Identify High-Focus Categories & Chemical Landscape

To pinpoint which product categories (e.g., makeup, skincare) have the highest volume of reported chemical exposures. We also aimed to evaluate the potential health effects by developing and applying a toxicity scoring system at the ingredient level.

2. Analyze Brand Behavior in Key Categories

To understand how different brands operate within the most reported product categories. This involved analyzing brand-specific report volumes and average hazard scores using visualizations like bar charts.

3. Investigate Co-occurrence of Hazardous Chemicals

To identify products containing multiple hazardous chemicals simultaneously and determine the subcategories where such co-occurrences are most common. This was explored using bar charts and heatmap concepts.

4. Temporal Clustering of Discontinuations & Reporting

To examine whether spikes in public chemical reporting align temporally with waves of product discontinuations. We used dual-line charts comparing trends in 'Initial Date Reported' and 'Discontinued Date'.

The Data: Insights from California's Safe Cosmetics Act

Dataset Overview

  • Source: Kaggle dataset "Toxic Beauty: Chemicals Exposed!" uploaded by Nafayun Noor.
  • Origin & Context: The data stems from California's landmark Safe Cosmetics Act (2005), which mandates companies to report products containing ingredients known or suspected to cause cancer, birth defects, or other reproductive harm.
  • Reliability: Derived from public data (likely regulatory/industry reports mandated by the Act), offering detailed chemical composition. Kaggle's platform adds a layer of community validation.
  • Time Period: Spans over a decade, from July 2009 to June 2020, capturing significant trends.
  • Scope: Contains approximately 114,635 entries.
  • Purpose: Primarily aimed at exposing potentially harmful chemicals in cosmetics sold in California, thereby increasing transparency for consumer safety and regulatory scrutiny.

Key Attributes & Profiling Insights

Core data fields include: ProductName, CompanyName, BrandName, PrimaryCategory, SubCategory, ChemicalName, CasNumber, InitialDateReported, MostRecentDateReported, DiscontinuedDate, ChemicalCount.

Profiling revealed: A vast diversity of products (33k+ unique names) and brands (2.7k+), yet concentration among fewer manufacturers (600+ companies). Products are well-categorized (13 primary, 89 subcategories). Most products report only one tracked chemical (mean ChemicalCount ~1.29), but some contain multiple.

Data Cleaning & Feature Engineering

To ensure data quality and prepare for analysis, we performed several steps:

  • Missing Values: Most columns had minimal missing data. Columns like `CSF`/`CSFID` were less critical. `DiscontinuedDate` missing values were handled by creating a status column. Crucially, rows missing `CasNumber` (chemical identifier) after attempting imputation were dropped (~4.5k rows) to maintain analysis integrity.
  • Brand Name Cleaning: Rows with missing `BrandName` were removed. Similar brand names (e.g., "L'Oreal" vs "L'Oreal Paris") were consolidated using string similarity (>95%) to avoid redundancy.
  • Data Types: Ensured all attributes (dates, numbers, text) were cast to appropriate types.
  • Status Column: Created `DiscontinuedStatus` ('Active'/'Discontinued') based on `DiscontinuedDate` presence.
  • Chemical Count Recalculation: The `ChemicalCount` was recalculated per unique product ID (`CDPHId`) to accurately reflect the number of *unique* reported chemicals per product.

Feature Engineering: Hazard Scoring

A key step was adding quantitative risk context:

  • Created "Hazard Score" (1-10, higher=worse) and "Hazard Level" (Low/Moderate/High) for the ~94 unique chemicals post-cleaning.
  • Scores based on intrinsic toxicity, primarily using the EWG Skin Deep® database.
  • Supplemented with data from other authoritative sources like IARC (cancer classification), California Prop 65 list, and EU/other regulatory bans/restrictions.

Key Findings & Visualizations

Finding 1: Makeup Dominates Chemical Reports, Led by Lip & Eye Products

Our analysis confirmed that Makeup Products (non-permanent) are the primary category for reported chemicals, with over 75,000 reports, far exceeding Nail Products (~15k) and Skin Care (~7.7k).

Within makeup, the highest chemical presence is found in:

  • Lip Products: Lipsticks, Liners, Pencils, Glosses (~15,100+ reports)
  • Eye Shadows (~13,700+ reports)
  • Foundations & Bases (~8,800+ reports)

Titanium Dioxide is overwhelmingly the most reported chemical (often used as a pigment/whitener), but its hazard score is low (1). However, several high-hazard chemicals are notable in makeup:

Notable High-Toxicity Chemicals Found in Makeup:

  • Retinol/Retinyl Esters (Score 9): Vitamin A derivatives. Concerns include potential for severe sunburn, skin irritation, and possibly UV-induced DNA damage if skin isn't protected. Risk increases significantly at higher concentrations (>0.3% OTC).
  • Benzophenone (Score 8): Used as a fragrance ingredient or UV absorber. Classified as a potential endocrine disruptor and possible human carcinogen (IARC Group 2B).
  • Cocamide Diethanolamine (Score 7): Foaming agent. Concerns about potential carcinogenicity, especially related to contamination during manufacturing.
  • Talc (Score 6 - depending on contamination): Common filler. Concerns primarily related to potential asbestos contamination, linked to respiratory issues and cancer.

Finding 2: Brand Landscape Shows Variation in Volume and Hazard

Analyzing brand behavior within the high-volume 'Makeup Products' category revealed significant differences:

  • High Volume Leaders: Sephora (4,638 reports in makeup), bareMinerals (2,899), and NYX (2,741) submitted the most reports for makeup products in the dataset.
  • Higher Average Hazard Scores: Among the top 15 reporting brands, NYX (Avg. Hazard Score: 4.85) and Sally Hansen (Avg. Hazard Score: 4.26) showed notably higher average hazard scores per reported product, suggesting formulations potentially relying more on higher-scoring chemicals.
  • Lower Hazard Despite Volume: Conversely, some brands with high report volumes, like Sephora (Avg. Hazard: ~1.6) and L'Oréal (Avg. Hazard: ~1.02), maintained relatively low average hazard scores. This could indicate different formulation strategies or use of lower-risk reported ingredients.
  • Luxury vs. Mass Market: The presence of both luxury (e.g., Dior, Charlotte Tilbury) and mass-market (e.g., Revlon, CoverGirl) brands shows the issue spans price points, though hazard profiles vary.

Finding 3: Hazardous Mixes Common in Eye Shadow, Foundations, Lip Gloss

A critical finding is that many products contain multiple reported chemicals (ChemicalCount ≥ 2) simultaneously, potentially leading to complex exposures. We identified the subcategories where this is most prevalent:

High-Risk Subcategories for Multiple Chemicals:

  • Eye Shadow: Stands out significantly with over 575 unique products containing two or more reported chemicals.
  • Foundations & Bases: ~273 products.
  • Lip Gloss/Shine: ~250 products.
  • Other notable categories include Lipsticks, Face Powders, and Blushes.

Co-occurrence Intensity (Examples based on Heatmap Analysis):

Visualizing co-occurrence intensity (like with a heatmap) reveals combinations:

  • Products containing highly toxic Toluene (Score 10) in Lip Gloss or Eye Shadow often contain an average of ~4 other reported chemicals.
  • Foundations containing Benzophenone-3 (Score 8) tend to co-occur with an average of ~3.7 other reported chemicals.
  • This "cocktail effect" means exposure isn't just to one chemical but potentially several simultaneously, complicating risk assessment.

Finding 4: Reporting vs. Discontinuation - No Clear Temporal Link Found

We investigated whether increased public reporting of chemicals in products led to subsequent waves of product discontinuations. The analysis compared trends over time (quarterly) for 'Initial Date Reported' vs. 'Discontinued Date'.

Key Observation:

Despite fluctuations in both reporting and discontinuations, no consistent correlation was observed. For example, a significant spike in discontinuations around 2013-2014 did not clearly follow a corresponding spike in initial product reporting in the preceding quarters.

Interpretation: This suggests that product discontinuation decisions are likely complex and influenced by a multitude of factors beyond just the public reporting requirements of the Safe Cosmetics Act. These factors could include internal brand strategies, ingredient availability, reformulation efforts, market trends, consumer feedback, or separate regulatory actions not captured in this specific reporting data.

Recommendations for Consumers

Based on our findings, here are practical steps consumers can take to navigate the cosmetics landscape more safely:

Be Aware of High-Risk Products

Exercise extra caution with makeup categories shown to have higher chemical reporting and co-occurrence rates: lipsticks, eye shadows, foundations, and lip glosses. Consider frequency of use and application area.

Scrutinize Ingredient Labels

Become familiar with and look out for high-hazard ingredients identified in our analysis (e.g., Toluene, BHA, high-concentration Retinoids, Benzophenone, certain Talc sources), especially in products used daily or over large skin areas. Use resources like EWG's Skin Deep® database to check specific ingredients.

Prefer Transparent Brands

Support companies that practice ingredient transparency by fully disclosing all components. Look for brands actively marketing "clean," "non-toxic," or "free-from" formulations, but always verify claims by checking the actual ingredient list.
